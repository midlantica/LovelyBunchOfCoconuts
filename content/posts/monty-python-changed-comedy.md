## How Monty Python Changed Comedy Forever

Before Monty Python's Flying Circus first aired on BBC One in October 1969, British comedy followed rules. There were setups and punchlines. There were characters and situations. There was a beginning, a middle, and an end. Comedy was a craft with a grammar, and you learned the grammar before you broke it.

Python burned the rulebook. Then they did a silly walk away from the ashes.

The six members — Graham Chapman, John Cleese, Terry Gilliam, Eric Idle, Terry Jones, and Michael Palin — had met at Oxford and Cambridge, where they had been doing something that British universities had always quietly encouraged: being extremely clever while pretending to be extremely stupid. Python took this tradition and weaponised it.

The Dead Parrot sketch is a masterclass in absurdist logic. The premise is simple: a man tries to return a dead parrot to a pet shop. The shopkeeper insists the parrot is not dead. What follows is not merely funny — it is philosophically rigorous. The sketch works because both parties are entirely committed to their position. There is no winking at the audience. The logic is followed to its conclusion with the seriousness of a legal argument.

The Spanish Inquisition sketch works on a different principle: the subversion of expectation, repeated until the repetition itself becomes the joke. Nobody expects the Spanish Inquisition. Nobody expects them to arrive a second time. Nobody expects them to arrive a third time on a bus, slightly out of breath.

The Ministry of Silly Walks is something else entirely — a piece of physical comedy so perfectly executed that it transcends the sketch format and becomes a kind of art. John Cleese's walk is not just funny. It is *correct*. It is exactly as silly as it needs to be, and not one degree sillier.

John Cleese once said: "Comedy always works best when it is serious about what it is doing." Python was always serious. That was the joke.

Their influence is everywhere: in The Simpsons' willingness to abandon narrative logic, in the surrealism of modern internet humour, in the very idea that comedy could be both intellectual and utterly, gloriously stupid at the same time. Monty Python's Spamalot ran for years on Broadway. The Dead Parrot sketch has been performed in the House of Commons.

They changed everything. They just did it while wearing silly hats.
