## Marginal Utility: Why the 10th Slice Matters Less

In the 1870s, economists discovered something obvious that revolutionized economics:

**The value of something depends on how much you already have.**

This is the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility.

The example:

You're starving. The first slice of pizza is worth everything.

The second slice? Still great.

The fifth slice? Pretty good.

The tenth slice? You might not even want it.

Same pizza. Different value. Why?

**Because value is marginal—it depends on the next unit, not the total.**

This destroyed the labor theory of value:

Marx said value comes from labor. So 10 slices should be worth 10 times one slice.

But they're not. The 10th slice is nearly worthless to you.

**Value isn't in the object. It's in your satisfaction from the next unit.**

This explains everything:

**Why water is cheap but diamonds are expensive:**

- Water is abundant. The marginal utility of one more gallon is low.
- Diamonds are scarce. The marginal utility of one more diamond is high.

**Why progressive taxation is economically illiterate:**

- The rich don't value their last dollar as much as the poor value theirs
- But that doesn't mean taking it creates more total utility
- You destroy the incentive to earn that dollar in the first place

**Why socialism fails:**

Central planners can't know your marginal utility. Only you know how much you value the next slice.

Marginal utility proves that value is personal, subjective, and impossible to centrally plan.

The left ignores this and pretends all units have equal value.

Economics says otherwise.
