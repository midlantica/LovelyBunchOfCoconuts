## The British Relationship with Tea

Tea is not a drink in Britain. This is the first thing you must understand. In other countries, tea is a beverage — a hot liquid consumed for pleasure or refreshment. In Britain, tea is a coping mechanism, a social ritual, a form of therapy, and, in extremis, a substitute for any emotional response that might otherwise require actual words.

"Shall I put the kettle on?" is the British response to: bereavement, divorce, redundancy, a minor household inconvenience, the outbreak of war, the end of a relationship, a disappointing sporting result, a slightly difficult Tuesday, and the news that the biscuits have run out. The question is not really about tea. The question is: *shall we acknowledge that something has happened and deal with it in the only way we know how?* The answer is always yes.

The British drink approximately 100 million cups of tea per day. This is not a statistic. It is a survival strategy.

The relationship between Britain and tea is, historically speaking, complicated. Tea came to Britain from China via the East India Company in the seventeenth century, and the British took to it with the enthusiasm of a people who had been waiting their entire national history for exactly this thing. They then proceeded to build an empire, at least partly, on the need to secure a reliable supply of it. The British Empire was not built on conquest, or trade, or the civilising mission, or any of the other explanations that have been offered. It was built on tea. Specifically, on the need to find more of it, grow more of it, and ensure that nothing — not geography, not climate, not the inconvenient sovereignty of other nations — stood between Britain and its next cup.

The correct way to make tea is a matter of national importance. Milk in first or milk in last? This question has ended friendships. It has divided families. It has been debated in the letters pages of newspapers for generations without resolution.

In the event of nuclear war, the British government's official advice is: make a cup of tea and wait for further instructions.
