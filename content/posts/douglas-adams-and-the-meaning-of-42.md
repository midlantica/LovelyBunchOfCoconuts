## Douglas Adams and the Meaning of 42

In *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*, a vast supercomputer called Deep Thought is asked to calculate the Answer to the Ultimate Question of Life, the Universe, and Everything. It takes seven and a half million years. The answer, when it finally arrives, is 42.

This is the funniest number in the universe. Douglas Adams chose it deliberately, and his explanation is itself a masterpiece of comic reasoning.

"It was a joke," Adams said. "It had to be a number, an ordinary, smallish number, and I chose that one. Binary representations, base thirteen, Tibetan monks — I sat at my desk, stared into the garden, and thought '42 will do.' I typed it out. End of story."

The genius of 42 is not in the number itself. The genius is in the structure of the joke. The joke is that the answer is meaningless without the question — and nobody knows the question. Deep Thought points out that you cannot understand the answer until you understand what was being asked. A second computer — the Earth itself — is built to calculate the question. It is destroyed by the Vogons five minutes before the answer is due.

This is, Adams suggested, the human condition. We have answers. We just don't know what the questions are. We have technology, philosophy, religion, science — all of them answers of a kind — and we are still not entirely sure what we were asking. The joke is not that the answer is 42. The joke is that we expected an answer at all.

Adams was a writer of rare intelligence who wore his intelligence lightly, which is the hardest thing to do. His comedy was always about something — about the absurdity of existence, the smallness of humanity in an indifferent universe, the gap between what we expect and what we get. He was also very funny about dolphins.

He died in 2001, at 49, which is not funny at all.

42. The answer to everything. The question to nothing.
