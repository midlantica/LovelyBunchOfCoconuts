## The Goon Show: How Three Men Invented British Comedy

Before The Goon Show, British radio comedy was polite. It was structured. It had characters who behaved like characters, plots that proceeded like plots, and jokes that arrived at the expected moment and departed cleanly. It was, in short, the kind of comedy that a nation recovering from a world war might produce: competent, reassuring, and entirely safe.

Then Spike Milligan, Peter Sellers, and Harry Secombe arrived, and nothing was safe again.

The Goon Show ran on BBC Radio from 1951 to 1960, and in those nine years it dismantled the conventions of British comedy with the cheerful thoroughness of men who had never been entirely convinced by those conventions in the first place. The plots were surreal. The characters were grotesque. The sound effects were anarchic. The logic — when there was logic — was the logic of a dream, or a nightmare, or a very specific kind of madness that Spike Milligan had apparently been carrying around since birth.

Milligan was the engine of the show — its writer, its driving force, its resident genius and its resident catastrophe. He wrote under conditions of extreme psychological pressure, producing scripts of extraordinary invention while simultaneously having what can only be described as a very difficult time being alive. The tension between his brilliance and his suffering is present in every episode.

Peter Sellers was something else: a man of almost no fixed identity who could become anyone, any voice, any character, with a completeness that was slightly unnerving. Harry Secombe was the warmth that held it together — the human element in a show that was frequently inhuman.

Their influence is immeasurable. John Lennon said The Goon Show was the biggest single influence on his sense of humour. Monty Python acknowledged the debt. Every British comedian who has ever done something strange and surreal and slightly mad owes something to three men in a BBC studio, making noises that had never been made on radio before.

The Goon Show ran from 1951 to 1960. In those nine years, Spike Milligan, Peter Sellers, and Harry Secombe invented the future of British comedy. They just didn't know it yet.
