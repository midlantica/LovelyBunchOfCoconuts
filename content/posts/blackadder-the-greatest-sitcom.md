## Blackadder: The Greatest Sitcom Ever Made

There are those who will argue for Fawlty Towers. There are those who will argue for Yes Minister. There are those who will argue for Only Fools and Horses, and they are not wrong to do so. But the case for Blackadder as the greatest British sitcom ever made is, in the end, unanswerable — because no other sitcom has ever done what Blackadder Goes Forth did in its final episode.

But we are getting ahead of ourselves.

What makes Blackadder remarkable is its reinvention. Each series placed the same essential character — cunning, sardonic, surrounded by idiots — in a completely different historical period. The first series gave us a dim medieval prince, stumbling through a court of equal dimness. The second reinvented him as a scheming Elizabethan courtier, sharp-tongued and magnificently vain. The third placed him in the Regency period as a butler of devastating intelligence serving a Prince Regent of devastating stupidity. Each reinvention was a creative risk. Each one worked.

The fourth series — Blackadder Goes Forth — set the character in the trenches of the First World War. Captain Blackadder, trapped in a mud-filled dugout, surrounded by the incompetence of his superiors and the cheerful obliviousness of Private Baldrick, spent six episodes trying to avoid going over the top. The comedy was as sharp as ever. The shadow of what was coming never lifted.

The final episode, *Goodbyeee*, ends with the characters going over the top. There is no last-minute reprieve. There is no joke to soften it. The guns fall silent. The screen fades to a field of poppies. It is one of the most devastating pieces of television ever made — and it works precisely because of everything that came before it. The comedy earned the tragedy.

Rowan Atkinson has said that the last episode was the most difficult thing he has ever done. You believe him.

Blackadder proved that comedy and tragedy are not opposites. They are the same thing, viewed from different distances.
