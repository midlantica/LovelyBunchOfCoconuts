## Say's Law: Supply Creates Its Own Demand

In 1803 Jean-Baptiste Say explained something that still confuses Keynesians:

**"Supply creates its own demand."**

Here's what he meant:

When a farmer produces wheat, he doesn't just create supply. He creates _purchasing power_.

He sells the wheat, earns money, and uses that money to buy shoes, tools, and clothes.

His supply of wheat creates demand for other goods.

This is Say's Law: Production creates income, which creates demand.

**You can't consume what hasn't been produced.**

The implications destroy Keynesian economics:

- "Stimulus" doesn't create prosperity—production does
- Government spending doesn't create demand—it redirects it
- Recessions aren't caused by "lack of demand"—they're caused by misallocated resources

Keynes tried to refute Say's Law by claiming demand can fall short of supply, causing unemployment.

Say's response: If people aren't buying your product, you're producing the wrong thing at the wrong price.

The solution isn't government spending. It's letting prices adjust so resources flow to what people actually want.

Say's Law explains why:

- Stimulus packages don't work
- "Cash for Clunkers" was a waste
- Printing money doesn't create wealth

Production creates prosperity. Government spending just moves it around.

Say was right in 1803. He's still right today.
