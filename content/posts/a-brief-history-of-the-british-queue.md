## A Brief History of the British Queue

The queue was not invented by the British. But it was perfected by them — and in perfecting it, they made it their own in the way that Britain has always made things its own: by insisting that their version is the only correct one and quietly judging everyone who does it differently.

The precise origins of the British queue are disputed by historians, but the most compelling theory places its invention in 1348, during the Black Death. As plague swept through England, killing roughly a third of the population, the British — even while dying — felt it would be unconscionably rude to push in. And so they queued. Patiently. In order of arrival. Because that is how it is done.

The queue is not merely a line. It is a moral framework. It is a statement of values, a declaration of civilisation, a quiet but firm insistence that we are better than this — better than the chaos, the shoving, the undignified scramble that lesser nations apparently find acceptable. The queue says: *I was here first, and I will wait my turn, and so will you, and if you don't, I will think very poorly of you indeed.*

Queue-jumping is not merely rude. It is a form of treason. There is no crime in the British social code more serious than pushing in. Murder, perhaps, might be forgiven in time. Queue-jumping is forever.

There is a story — possibly apocryphal, certainly true in spirit — of a British regiment during the First World War that, upon receiving orders to retreat, formed an orderly queue to do so. The enemy, reportedly, was so confused that they paused their advance entirely. The British retreated in good order, on time, and without incident. The queue had saved the day.

Today, the queue endures. At bus stops, at post offices, at the deli counter, at the gates of heaven itself — the British queue. They queue in rain. They queue in sunshine. They queue when there is no discernible reason to queue, simply because a queue has formed and it would be wrong not to join it.

Other nations have armies, navies, and air forces. Britain has the queue.
