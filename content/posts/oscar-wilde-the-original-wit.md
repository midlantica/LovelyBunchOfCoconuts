## Oscar Wilde: The Original Wit

There is a test for a great epigram: it should feel, upon first hearing, not like something that has been invented, but like something that has always existed — a truth so perfectly expressed that it seems impossible it was ever not said. By this measure, Oscar Wilde is the greatest wit in the English language. His epigrams do not feel composed. They feel discovered.

"I can resist everything except temptation." This is not merely funny. It is a precise and accurate description of the human condition, delivered in eleven words with the elegance of a mathematical proof.

"The truth is rarely pure and never simple." This is philosophy disguised as wit — or perhaps wit disguised as philosophy. Either way, it is correct.

"Work is the curse of the drinking classes." This is the inversion of a Victorian moral platitude — "drink is the curse of the working classes" — executed with such precision that the original sounds clumsy by comparison.

Wilde understood something that lesser wits miss: the epigram is not merely about being funny. It is about being *true*. The laugh comes from recognition — from the sudden, slightly uncomfortable realisation that this is exactly right, that you have always known this, that you simply lacked the words. Wilde had the words. He always had the words.

His plays — *The Importance of Being Earnest*, *An Ideal Husband*, *Lady Windermere's Fan* — are machines for the delivery of epigrams, and they are magnificent machines. But Wilde was also a serious artist, a man who understood that comedy was not a lesser form than tragedy, but its equal.

His tragedy — imprisoned for two years for the crime of being who he was, his health broken, his career destroyed — makes his comedy all the more remarkable. He continued to be funny, even in ruin. He continued to be precise, even in despair.

Wilde's greatest joke was on the society that destroyed him. He is remembered. They are not.
