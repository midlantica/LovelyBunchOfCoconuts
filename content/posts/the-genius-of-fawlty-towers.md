## The Genius of Fawlty Towers

Twelve episodes. That is all there is. Twelve episodes of Fawlty Towers, broadcast across two series in 1975 and 1979, and then nothing. No reunion specials. No reboots. No extended universe. Just twelve perfect half-hours of the finest farce ever committed to television, and then silence.

The restraint is itself a form of genius.

Fawlty Towers was written by John Cleese and his then-wife Connie Booth, who had the remarkable discipline to stop when they had said everything they had to say. Most television shows continue until they have said everything twice, then three times, then until the audience has stopped listening entirely. Cleese and Booth understood something most writers never learn: the exit is part of the performance.

The show's central character, Basil Fawlty, is a monster. He is a snob of operatic proportions, a man of catastrophic incompetence who has somehow ended up running a hotel in Torquay, a situation that suits neither him nor his guests. He despises his guests. He despises his staff. He despises his wife, Sybil, though he is also terrified of her, which is the correct response. He is rude, dishonest, and spectacularly bad at everything he attempts.

And yet we love him. This is the miracle of the show.

Cleese has said: "The show is about a man who should not be running a hotel." This is true, but it understates the case. Basil Fawlty should not be running anything. He should not be trusted with a houseplant. The comedy comes from watching a man of vast self-regard and minimal competence attempt to maintain the fiction that he is in control — and from watching that fiction collapse, episode by episode, in increasingly elaborate ways.

The construction of each episode is extraordinary. Every lie compounds the previous lie. Every attempt to fix a problem creates three new problems. The farce builds with the precision of a Swiss watch — a Swiss watch that is also somehow on fire.

Twelve episodes. No more, no less. Fawlty Towers understood something most shows never learn: leave them wanting more.
