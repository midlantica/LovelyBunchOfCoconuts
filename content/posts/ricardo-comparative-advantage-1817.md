## Ricardo's Comparative Advantage

In 1817 David Ricardo published _On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation_ and proved something that still blows people's minds:

**Even if one country is better at producing everything, both countries still benefit from trade.**

His example:

England produces cloth cheaper than Portugal.

Portugal produces wine cheaper than England.

Easy case—they should trade, right?

But what if Portugal is better at producing _both_ cloth and wine?

Protectionists say: "Portugal should make everything itself!"

Ricardo showed they're wrong.

Even if Portugal is better at both, it should still specialize in whatever it's _most_ better at (wine), and trade for cloth.

Why? Because **opportunity cost** matters more than absolute advantage.

Every hour Portugal spends making cloth is an hour _not_ making wine—where it has an even bigger edge.

The result: Both nations get more of both goods through trade than either could produce alone.

This is **comparative advantage**—the single most counterintuitive, most important, and most ignored insight in all of economics.

It destroys every argument for tariffs, trade wars, and economic nationalism.

Protectionists have spent 200 years pretending Ricardo never existed.

He did. And he was right.
