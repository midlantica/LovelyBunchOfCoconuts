## Bill of Rights: Limits on Government

In 1791 the Bill of Rights was ratified.

Most people misunderstand it.

The Bill of Rights doesn't _grant_ rights. It _protects_ rights you already have.

**It tells government what it cannot do.**

### Read the language:

- "Congress shall make no law..." (1st Amendment)
- "...shall not be infringed" (2nd Amendment)
- "...shall not be violated" (4th Amendment)

These aren't permissions. They're prohibitions.

The Founders knew that government is the greatest threat to liberty.

So they built a fence around our rights:

**1st Amendment**: Government can't control speech, religion, press, or assembly.

**2nd Amendment**: Government can't disarm the people.

**4th Amendment**: Government can't search you without cause.

**5th Amendment**: Government can't take your property or force self-incrimination.

**10th Amendment**: Powers not given to the federal government belong to states and people.

The pattern is clear: The Bill of Rights limits government, not citizens.

The left treats rights as gifts from government that can be regulated, limited, or revoked.

The Founders knew better: Rights are inherent. Government's job is to protect them—not grant them.

When government claims the power to give rights, it claims the power to take them away.

The Bill of Rights says: No.
