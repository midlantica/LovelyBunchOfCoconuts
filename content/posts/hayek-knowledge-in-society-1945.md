## The Use of Knowledge in Society

Published in the American Economic Review, September 1945.

This is the essay everyone quotes when they say "the knowledge-problem argument" or "Hayek's knowledge problem."

Key lines that changed economics forever:

- "The economic problem of society is not merely a problem of how to allocate 'given' resources… it is a problem of the **utilization of knowledge which is not given to anyone in its totality**."

- Most of the knowledge we need to coordinate a modern economy is **not** scientific data in textbooks.

It is local, fleeting, tacit:

- a factory manager knows his machines are wearing out

- a trader knows copper is suddenly scarce in one port

- a housewife knows she prefers more fruit this month

- No central planner can ever collect this dispersed, changing, often unarticulated knowledge.

- The price system solves it automatically: a higher copper price instantly tells thousands of producers and users around the world "economise or find substitutes" — without anyone needing to know _why_ the price rose.

Hayek's conclusion is devastating for central planning:

"The marvel is that in a case like that of the scarcity of one raw material, without an order being issued, without more than perhaps a few people knowing the cause, tens of thousands of people… are made to use the material or its products more sparingly; that is, they move in the right direction."

Mises showed socialism can't calculate.

Hayek showed that even if it somehow could, it still couldn't **discover** what needs to be calculated.

This 1945 essay (just 11 pages) is the single most cited and most lethal attack on central planning ever written.
